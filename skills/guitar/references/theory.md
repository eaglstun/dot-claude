# Music Theory Reference — OWNER/OPERATORS Charts

This file supports the guitar skill with harmonic context, specifically for layering jazzier chord colors onto songs that are fundamentally rock. Read it alongside SKILL.md.

---

## Diatonic foundation

Every song lives in a key. The key tells you which triads are "native" (diatonic) — they all share the same scale. In a major key:

```
Scale degree:  I    ii   iii  IV   V    vi   vii°
Chord quality: maj  min  min  maj  maj  min  dim
```

In a minor key (natural minor / Aeolian):

```
Scale degree:  i    ii°  III  iv   v    VI   VII
Chord quality: min  dim  maj  min  min  maj  maj
```

Most OWNER/OPERATORS songs use the relative major/minor pair — the same seven notes, different tonal center. E major and C# minor share the same key signature; so do A major and F# minor, D major and B minor.

**Common keys in the catalog:**

| Song                | Key                | Center                       |
| ------------------- | ------------------ | ---------------------------- |
| Breaker Breaker     | D minor / F major  | Dm                           |

| Deadpan Clean       | F# minor / A major | F#m (verse), B (chorus)      |
| Digital Love Glitch | A major / A minor  | Am (verse), A (bridge/outro) |

## The rock baseline — when plain triads are right

Power chords and open triads define the rock baseline. They work because they're unambiguous in dense mixes, rhythmically gripping, and emotionally direct. Use them for high-energy sections where the chord needs to land hard: choruses with big riffs, verses with driving rhythms, anywhere the lyric or beat is already doing the heavy lifting.

## Colorizing techniques — adding jazz without leaving rock

### sus2 and sus4 — float and ambiguity

Suspend the 3rd. The chord hovers, not major or minor, unresolved.

- **sus2** (root + 2nd + 5th): open, airy, slightly melancholic — `Dsus2`, `Gsus2`, `Asus2`, `Csus2`
- **sus4** (root + 4th + 5th): more tense, wants to resolve down — `Gsus4`, `Esus4`

`A(7)sus4` adds the minor 7th, making it a dominant-sus chord — floaty but with pull (_More Than Enough_ intro).

**Where to reach for it:** trailing bars of a section, pre-chorus float, any moment where the lyric is uncertain or searching.

---

### add9 — warmth without complexity

Add the 9th (the 2nd an octave up) to a triad without adding a 7th. Brighter than a plain major, without the jazz weight of a maj7. Stacks cleanly over rock rhythms.

- `Eadd9`, `Dadd9`, `Aadd9`, `Bmadd9`, `Eadd9/G#`

In _Echoes and Static_, `Eadd9` carries the chorus lift (alternating with `Dmaj7`), feeling warmer and more open than a plain E. `Bmadd9/F#` in the outro adds luminosity to a minor chord without darkening it the way Bm7 would.

**Where to reach for it:** choruses that need emotional expansion, any IV or V chord that feels too blunt.

---

### 6th chords — brightness without heaviness

6th chords (root + 3rd + 5th + 6th) are lighter than 7th chords — closer to a triad, more pop than jazz.

- `E6`, `A6`, `A6/F#`, `B6`

`E6` appears throughout _Post-Truth Light_ and _Echoes and Static_ as a warm resolution point — the final chord of a 4-bar loop, landing with a sense of arrival brighter than a plain E. `A6/F#` keeps the A harmony but shifts the bass to F#, creating a descending bass line effect.

**Where to reach for it:** cadences (especially the I chord at the end of a phrase), any major chord that needs shine rather than weight.

---

### maj7 — lush and cinematic

Add the major 7th to a major triad. The major 7th sits a half-step below the root, creating gentle internal tension — lush, floating, not driving.

- `Dmaj7`, `Amaj7`, `Dmaj7/F#`

`Dmaj7` is used in _Echoes and Static_ chorus as an arrival chord after the tension of `Eadd9` — a breath. `Amaj7` in _Post-Truth Light_ intro softens the opening loop immediately. `Dmaj7/F#` in Break 1 creates a glassy, suspended texture over the F# bass.

**Where to reach for it:** intros, instrumental sections, bridge wide-open moments, any IV chord that should feel luxuriant rather than driving.

---

### Minor 7ths — dark groove

Add the minor 7th to a minor triad. This is the most jazz-rock of all the extensions — deep, sophisticated, and grooving.

- `Em7`, `C#m7`, `Bm7`, `F#m7`

The b7 gives a minor chord that extra layer of darkness without dissonance. `Em7` in _Heart With Handles_ pre-chorus 2 substitutes for plain Em — the 7th softens the pull and makes it linger.

**Where to reach for it:** pre-choruses, verses that want sophistication, any vi chord where you want depth instead of starkness.

---

### Dominant 7ths — blues and tension

Add the minor 7th to a major triad. The tritone between the 3rd and b7 wants to resolve, pulling hard toward the I chord.

- `A7/D`, `B7`, `G#7`, `C7`, `F#(7)`

`A7/D` in _Echoes and Static_ Break 1 sits over a D bass — a jazz move (A7 = V of D), with the D pedal making it grooving rather than just tense. `G#7` in _Post-Truth Light_ bridge is a secondary dominant (V7 of C#m), creating a sharp pull before the bridge resolution.

**Where to reach for it:** bridge tension, the chord before a big resolution, anywhere a major chord needs blues grit.

---

### Slash chords and voice leading

Slash chords (`chord/bass`) put a specific bass note under a chord that isn't the root. Two main uses:

**Smooth bass line movement** — the upper chord stays while the bass walks:

- `Dmaj7/F#` — D chord, F# in bass (first inversion, lighter than root position)
- `A6/F#` — A chord, F# in bass (jazz inversion)
- `E6/G#`, `Eadd9/G#` — E harmony over G# bass (the 3rd — brightens the voicing)
- `D/F#` in _More Than Enough_ verse 2 — D chord, bass walks E → F# → G

**Pedal point** — static bass while upper voices move:

- `A7/D` + `F#7sus4/D` back-to-back (_Echoes and Static_ Break 1) — completely different upper structures, both over a D pedal, creating a floating jazzy vamp
- `G#m/B · A/C#` in _Post-Truth Light_ verse tail — ascending bass line (B → C#) under changing upper chords

**Where to reach for it:** any transition where root-to-root motion sounds clunky, bridge sections, bass-led intros, when you want a melodic bass line without writing a full bass part.

---

### Extended chords — 9, 11, b9, b13

These go beyond the 7th. Place them deliberately — they're the most harmonically complex.

**9th and 11th** (add degrees on top of a 7th chord):

- `F#m7(9,11)` — F#m7 with added 9th (G#) and 11th (B). Very open, modal. Used in _Post-Truth Light_ instrumental as the song opens into a wide cinematic passage.
- `C#m7(9,b13)` — C#m7 with a 9th and flat 13th (A = the minor 6th of C#). The b13 is borrowed from C# Dorian. Dark and complex.

**Flat 9** — the altered dominant:

- `A7(b9)/Eb` in _Breaker Breaker_ Bridge A is the most "jazz" chord in the catalog. Dominant 7 with the 9th lowered a half-step, maximum dissonance. The Eb bass is the tritone of A (a tritone substitution bass). Function: V7(b9) → i, the darkest possible cadence into Dm.

**b13** — Dorian / jazz minor color:

- `G#mb13/E` in _Post-Truth Light_ — G#m with a flat 13th (E natural, borrowed from G# Dorian), over an E bass. Deliberately ambiguous — effectively an Em7 with the 3rd in the upper voice.

**Where to reach for it:** instrumental sections, outros, bridges at their peak complexity, anywhere the song needs to open up harmonically beyond the rock changes.

---

### Diminished chords — passing tension

Diminished chords (every interval a minor 3rd) are pure tension. They most often function as passing chords — a half-step approach to the next chord.

- `Bdim`, `A#dim`, `F#dim`, `A#dim/F#`

`Bdim` in _Heart With Handles_ verse 2 (bar 48) replaces what would be a plain C — the diminished adds a dark wobble just before the pre-chorus. `A#dim/F#` is essentially a D7 without the root, pulling hard toward B minor or E major.

**Where to reach for it:** passing chord between two diatonic chords (especially moving up by half-step), the last bar of a verse before a dramatic chorus.

## Borrowed chords — raiding parallel keys

When a chord doesn't belong to the home key, it's borrowed. Common examples in the catalog:

- `G#7` in E major: borrowed from E harmonic minor, where G# carries the leading-tone dominant color
- `Gno3` (_More Than Enough_): a bVII chord in an E minor context — the most common modal borrow in rock; dropping the 3rd removes its major quality and leaves it as a raw open fifth
- Secondary dominants: `B7` in an E major song (V of V), `G#7` going to C#m (V7 of iii)

## Practical heuristics — when to reach for jazz color

| Section type                | Lean toward                                          |
| --------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Main chorus, big riff       | Plain triads — don't fight the impact                |
| Verse with space            | add9, sus2, maj7 — add air                           |
| Pre-chorus building         | sus4, dominant 7ths — increase tension               |
| Bridge / B section          | Extended chords, slash chords with voice leading     |
| Instrumental break          | Full jazz palette — 9ths, 11ths, b13s, slash pedals  |
| Outro / fade                | maj7, 6ths, sus2 — open and unresolved               |
| Transition between sections | Slash chord with bass line, diminished passing chord |

The heavier the rhythm, the simpler the chord. Jazz chords breathe best in open-space arrangements. A `C#m7(9,b13)` disappears under a wall of guitars but transforms a song during an arpeggiated instrumental passage.

## Quick substitution table

When a chart has a plain chord that could be jazzier, these are common safe swaps:

| Plain chord                        | Jazzier substitute             | Effect                                 |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------ | -------------------------------------- |
| I (major)                          | Imaj7                          | Warmer, more floating                  |
| I (major)                          | I6 or Iadd9                    | Brighter, still light                  |
| IV (major)                         | IVmaj7                         | Lush resolution                        |
| V (major)                          | V7 or Vsus4                    | More tension, cleaner push             |
| vi (minor)                         | vim7                           | Deeper groove, jazz feel               |
| ii (minor)                         | iim7 or iim7(9,11)             | Opens up; very smooth in slow passages |
| Any major chord moving up by step  | Diminished passing chord       | Half-step chromatic tension            |
| Root-position chord over root bass | Same chord, 3rd in bass (/3rd) | Smoother bass line                     |
